# First footsteps

I'm Andrei, and this blog is where I'm going to write about things that matter to me both technical and not, and I'm going to assume some of it might matter to you too, reader.

I was born in 2001 in Craiova, Romania, and throughout my entire life there's been one thing that never changed: a relentless ***curiosity***. I needed to know, to understand, and to share everything I learned.

From preschool days where I'd cry before sleep because I couldn't finish the "human body explained" magazine before the next issue arrived, through the endless stream of **Why? Why? ... But why?** questions that I'm sure drove my parents absolutely mad, I felt this compulsion to be aware of what was happening in the world. From how the universe began to volcanoes erupting to the human body to dinosaurs to video games to books to building things with my own hands, I was always learning.

## Adolescence, sport and depression

Through gymnasium and early adolescence, I tried every sport under the sun and for sure I wasn't particularly talented at any of them, but more importantly, they weren't doing anything to help me lose weight. I was an obese kid, 70kg at 155cm in 7th grade, getting bullied relentlessly. The world, which had been full of color, turned gray. I was constantly on the edge, and what pulled me back was finally losing the weight in 10th grade and working with a high school therapist who helped me understand that being a **good person** is infinitely more important than anything else. From that point on, I steadily improved.

## Internship in 11th grade

I wanted to try something before my last year, so I spotted an internship at a company founded in my hometown. I knew some HTML, CSS, and JS, so I threw together a CV, applied, built the home assignment, a todo app, and somehow got hired.

Then I worked my ass off, learned Vue, Java, and PHP making a product price comparison between different providers based on scraping, and from that point forward, I knew exactly what I was supposed to be doing. That internship taught me that building software wasn't just about writing code, it was about solving real problems.

## Started university and got hired

During the pandemic I took my final exams and got admitted to university to study computer science. In parallel, I wanted to work, to earn money, to be independent, so I took a full-time job as a fullstack dev building a patient-facing portal for COVID registrations for a US company.

I deeply learned Vue, Javascript, PHP, MySQL, and got to lead a team. We built features like decision tree systems, modular plugin architectures, and secure payment gateways, handling peak loads exceeding 1 million user interactions per month. I learned a hell of a lot, probably more than I should have at that pace.

Then I got promoted to Team Lead Developer, mentored two junior engineers, and managed a team that grew from 4 to 6 members. We orchestrated development across diverse projects with varied technology stacks, and our client base doubled. Later I became a Senior Fullstack Engineer, working on clinical studies in US Healthcare with strict compliance requirements like 21CFR-Part 11 and HIPAA. I engineered audit trails, implemented security middlewares, and led a UI/UX overhaul.

## A twist and where I am now

Fast forward, I finished university and was becoming a decent engineer, moved to Timisoara with my fiancée and got fired from my last job due to misunderstandings with my boss at the time. ***It wasn't the ending I expected, but sometimes that's how life goes.***

*I applied everywhere, went back to basic, to strengthen the foundation, but no one seemed to hire people with less than 5 years of experience. I was desperate and contemplating on* **letting all go.**

Then, a ray of hope appeared, a company accepted my application. I interviewed at d.velop and passed with flying colors. Now I'm a senior frontend engineer working with Svelte, developing a Contract Management System using microservices architecture. I'm deep in component design and structural implementation, establishing consistent design systems and reusable UI components, implementing Material Design principles to create intuitive, accessible user interfaces.

And when I'm not doing that, I experiment with a lot of technical stuff as I am on a continous improvement journey.

***Stay tuned, more coming.***

### Lessons to be learned

- Life isn't always as bleak as it appears, there's always a way to see it in a brighter light
- If you do your best, you're going to succeed, that's just how it works
- Kaizen, a japanese way of thinking focusing on continous improvement
- Experiment, take risks, actually live your life
- Be a good person, everything else is secondary
